Over any V7♭9, you can play a diminished 7 arpeggio rooted on the ♭9 of the
chord. The four notes outline the upper structure of the dominant — pick a V7
root below to see the shape on strings 2–3–4.

<DimOverDominants />

The dim7 from the ♭9 spells out the **3, 5, ♭7, ♭9** of the dominant. Skipping
the root keeps the line ambiguous and tense — exactly what a V chord wants
before resolution. Over G7♭9, A♭°7 (A♭ B D F) gives you B, D, F, A♭ — the 3,
5, ♭7, and ♭9 of G7.

Because the diminished 7 chord is built in stacked minor 3rds, the same shape
repeats every three frets on these strings. Over one V7♭9 you have four
interchangeable starting points across the neck — pick whichever lies under
your hand.

## Chord positions

The same symmetry applies to the dim7 chord shape. Pick a V7 root and a
string set — strings 1-2-3-4 (top four) or 2-3-4-5 (middle four) — and
because every chord tone can act as the root, the shape repeats every three
frets up the neck.

Each diagram is the same chord — only the bass note changes. Comp it as a
substitute for the V7♭9 itself, or as a punchy stab on beats 2 and 4 leading
into the resolution.
