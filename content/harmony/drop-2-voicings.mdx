Drop 2 voicings are built by taking a close-voiced four-note chord and dropping
the **second voice from the top** down an octave. This creates a wider voicing
that sits naturally on four adjacent guitar strings.

Each chord quality has four inversions — root position, 1st, 2nd, and 3rd — and
each can be played on three different string sets.

## Understanding drop 2 in depth

### What is a close voicing?

Before understanding drop 2, you need to know what a **close voicing** (also
called a close position or closed voicing) is. A close voicing is a chord where
all four notes are packed within one octave, stacked in thirds:

> **Cmaj7 close voicing:** C – E – G – B (all within a single octave)

On piano this is easy to play. On guitar, however, close voicings are
impractical — cramming four notes into one octave often requires awkward
stretches or impossible fingerings.

### The drop 2 technique

Drop 2 voicings solve this problem with one simple operation:

1. Start with a close-voiced four-note chord (four voices stacked top to bottom)
2. Count from the top: the **second voice from the top** is "dropped" — moved
   **down one octave**
3. The result is a wider, open voicing that spans more than an octave

For example, with **Cmaj7** in close position (top to bottom: **B – G – E – C**):

| Voice | Close position | After drop 2 |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| 1 (top) | B | B |
| 2 | G | ↓ dropped one octave |
| 3 | E | E |
| 4 (bottom) | C | C |
| New bottom | — | G (dropped from voice 2) |

The result **B – E – C – G** gives us an open, spread voicing that falls
naturally across four adjacent guitar strings.

### Why drop 2 voicings matter for guitar

- **Playable shapes** — the spread interval structure fits the guitar's tuning,
  landing cleanly on four consecutive strings without extreme stretches
- **Rich harmonic sound** — the wider spacing creates depth and clarity that
  close voicings lack, each note rings distinctly
- **Voice leading** — drop 2 inversions connect smoothly with minimal finger
  movement, making chord progressions sound fluid
- **Versatility** — the same shapes work across jazz, bossa nova, R&B, gospel,
  and any style that uses seventh chords
- **Three string sets** — each voicing can be played on strings 6-5-4-3,
  5-4-3-2, or 4-3-2-1, giving you options across the entire neck register

### Four inversions

Each drop 2 chord has **four inversions**, determined by which chord tone is the
lowest note:

| Inversion | Bottom note | Voice order (low → high) |
|-----------|-----------|------------------------|
| Root position | Root (R) | R – 5 – 7 – 3 |
| 1st inversion | Third (3) | 3 – 7 – R – 5 |
| 2nd inversion | Fifth (5) | 5 – R – 3 – 7 |
| 3rd inversion | Seventh (7) | 7 – 3 – 5 – R |

Learning all four inversions across all three string sets gives you **12 shapes
per chord quality** — a complete map of the fretboard.

### Practice tips

- **Learn one quality at a time** — start with maj7 or m7 and master all four
  inversions before moving on
- **Connect inversions** — play root → 1st → 2nd → 3rd inversion going up the
  neck on the same string set; this builds your fretboard awareness
- **Move across string sets** — once comfortable on one string set, shift the
  same inversions to adjacent string sets to hear the timbral differences
- **Apply to progressions** — once you know the shapes, head to the
  [ii-V-I voice leading](/harmony/drop-2-ii-v-i) page to practice connecting
  chords with smooth voice movement
