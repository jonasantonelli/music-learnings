**Harmonic minor** is a natural minor scale with a raised 7th. That single
change — raising the ♭7 to a natural 7 — creates the leading tone needed to
build a true **V7 chord** in a minor key, which is why classical harmony uses
it to power the V-i cadence. The side effect is a 3-semitone **augmented
second** between the ♭6 and the natural 7, which gives the scale its
distinctive "exotic" or "Eastern" flavor.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | ♭3 | 4 | 5 | ♭6 | 7 |
|--------|---|---|----|---|---|----|---|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 8 | 11 |

Compared to natural minor (1 2 ♭3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7), only the 7th is raised.
Compared to melodic minor, it keeps the ♭6. The gap between ♭6 and 7 is 3
semitones — that's the augmented 2nd.

### The modes of harmonic minor

Like melodic minor, harmonic minor's real value is as a parent scale for a
family of modes, several of which are very commonly used:

| Degree | Mode name | Common use |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Harmonic Minor | Minor(maj7) — classical minor cadence |
| 2 | Locrian ♮6 | Half-diminished with natural 6 |
| 3 | Ionian ♯5 | Maj7♯5 chords |
| 4 | Dorian ♯4 | Minor chords with a ♯11 color |
| 5 | [Mixolydian ♭9 ♭13](/lessons/scales/mixolydian-b9-b13) | V7 resolving to minor i |
| 6 | Lydian ♯2 | Maj7♯9 flavor |
| 7 | Altered Diminished | Diminished 7 chords |

### Chord applications

The harmonic minor scale itself is used for:

- **The V7 chord in a minor ii-V-i** — *but* played from the i, not from the
  V. If you're resolving to A minor, the V7 is E7. Playing A harmonic minor
  *over* that E7 gives you the 5th mode of A harmonic minor, which is
  Mixolydian ♭9♭13 — see its [dedicated page](/lessons/scales/mixolydian-b9-b13).
- **Minor(maj7) chords** — like melodic minor, harmonic minor contains the
  natural 7 needed for m(maj7)
- **Standalone "classical" or flamenco minor melodies** — the augmented 2nd
  between ♭6 and 7 is the signature sound of flamenco, Middle Eastern, and
  neoclassical metal music

### Practice tips

- **Hear the augmented 2nd** — play the scale slowly and listen for the
  dramatic leap between the ♭6 and 7. That's the sound you're buying
- **Connect V7 to its minor i** — over an Am chord, play E7 and follow it
  with a line built from A harmonic minor to hear why this scale gives the
  "minor cadence" its pull
- **Learn the 5th mode first** — most jazz players use harmonic minor as a
  way to reach Phrygian Dominant, which is where the practical value lives
- **Don't confuse it with melodic minor** — the difference is just the 6th
  (♭6 here, natural 6 in melodic minor), but it completely changes the flavor
