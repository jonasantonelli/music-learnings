**Locrian** is the 7th mode of the [major scale](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/ionian) and the
darkest of all seven Greek modes. Its defining feature is the **♭5** — no other
major-scale mode has a diminished 5th. This makes the tonic chord a diminished
triad (or m7♭5), which means Locrian has no stable resting point. It's
unstable by nature.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | ♭2 | ♭3 | 4 | ♭5 | ♭6 | ♭7 |
|--------|---|----|----|---|----|----|-----|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 1 | 3 | 5 | 6 | 8 | 10 |

Compared to Aeolian (natural minor), both the 2nd and the 5th are lowered.
The ♭5 is the critical one — it turns the tonic triad into a diminished chord.

### Chord applications

- **Half-diminished chords (m7♭5)** — Locrian is the default scale for the vii
  chord in a major key and the ii chord in a minor ii-V-i
- **Minor ii-V-i** — when you see Am7♭5–D7–Gm, Locrian is the starting point
  for the Am7♭5 (though many players prefer
  [Locrian ♮2](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/melodic-minor) from melodic minor for its
  smoother sound)

### Practice tips

- **Hear the ♭5** — play B Locrian over a Bm7♭5 chord and sustain the F
  natural; that diminished 5th is what makes it sound so unstable
- **Use it in context** — Locrian is rarely used modally for long stretches;
  it's most useful within a ii-V-i in minor
- **Compare with Locrian ♮2** — in jazz, the 6th mode of melodic minor (Locrian
  with a natural 2 instead of ♭2) is often preferred because the natural 2
  avoids the ♭9 clash with the root
- **Know the parent** — B Locrian = C major starting from B; this relationship
  makes it easy to find the notes
