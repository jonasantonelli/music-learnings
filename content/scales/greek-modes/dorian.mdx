**Dorian** is the 2nd mode of the [major scale](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/ionian). It's a
minor scale with a natural 6th instead of a ♭6, which gives it a brighter,
less melancholic sound than natural minor. It's the default scale choice for
minor 7th chords in jazz and the backbone of countless modal tunes — from
"So What" to "Oye Como Va."

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | ♭3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | ♭7 |
|--------|---|---|----|---|---|---|----|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 9 | 10 |

Compared to Aeolian (natural minor), the only difference is the natural 6
instead of ♭6. That single note is the Dorian "color tone."

### Chord applications

- **Minor 7th chords (m7)** — Dorian is the default scale for the ii chord in
  a major ii-V-I and for any minor 7th chord that isn't functioning as a vi
- **Minor 6th and minor 13th chords** — the natural 6 is what allows these
  chord extensions
- **Modal minor vamps** — when a tune sits on one minor chord for several bars,
  Dorian's brightness keeps it from sounding too dark

### Practice tips

- **Hear the natural 6** — play D Dorian over a Dm7 chord and linger on the B
  natural; compare it with D Aeolian where that note is B♭
- **Use it over ii chords** — in a Dm7–G7–Cmaj7 progression, Dorian is the
  natural choice on the Dm7
- **Connect to its parent** — D Dorian is C major starting from D; be able to
  think both ways instantly
- **Compare with Aeolian** — the ♭6 vs. natural 6 distinction is subtle but
  changes everything about the mood
