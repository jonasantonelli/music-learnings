The **Ionian** mode is the major scale itself — the foundation of Western
harmony. Every other mode is measured against it: "♭3" means lower than
Ionian's 3rd, "♯4" means higher than Ionian's 4th, and so on. It's the
brightest of the seven Greek modes, with no altered tones to create tension.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
|--------|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 4 | 5 | 7 | 9 | 11 |

All intervals are "natural" — this is the template that defines what
"natural" means.

### The seven Greek modes

Ionian is the parent scale that generates the seven modes of the major scale.
Each mode starts on a different degree but uses the same pool of notes:

| Degree | Mode name | Quality |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | **Ionian** | Major |
| 2 | [Dorian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/dorian) | Minor |
| 3 | [Phrygian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/phrygian) | Minor |
| 4 | [Lydian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/lydian) | Major |
| 5 | [Mixolydian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/mixolydian) | Dominant |
| 6 | [Aeolian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/aeolian) | Minor |
| 7 | [Locrian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/locrian) | Diminished |

### Chord applications

- **Maj7 chords** — Ionian is the default scale over any I chord in a major key
- **I–IV–V progressions** — the backbone of pop, rock, and country harmony
- **Avoid note: the 4th** — over a static Maj7 chord, the natural 4 creates a
  half-step clash against the 3rd and is traditionally treated as a passing tone

### Practice tips

- **Learn it as the reference** — every other mode is defined by what it changes
  relative to Ionian, so knowing it cold is essential
- **Hear the avoid note** — hold a Cmaj7 chord and sustain an F over it to hear
  the tension the natural 4 creates
- **Compare with Lydian** — play Ionian then Lydian back to back; the only
  difference is the ♯4, but the entire mood shifts
