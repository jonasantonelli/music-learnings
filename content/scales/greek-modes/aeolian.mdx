**Aeolian** is the 6th mode of the [major scale](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/ionian) and is
more commonly known as the **natural minor** scale. It's the foundation of minor
key harmony — the scale that defines what "minor" sounds like. When musicians
say "A minor" without further qualification, they mean A Aeolian.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | ♭3 | 4 | 5 | ♭6 | ♭7 |
|--------|---|---|----|---|---|----|-----|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 8 | 10 |

Compared to Ionian, the 3rd, 6th, and 7th are all lowered. The ♭3 makes it
minor; the ♭6 gives it its characteristic melancholy; the ♭7 means there's
no leading tone pulling to the root.

### Chord applications

- **Minor chords in a minor key** — Aeolian is the default over i, iv, and v
  chords in natural minor harmony
- **vi chord in a major key** — Am7 in C major naturally produces A Aeolian
- **Pop and rock minor progressions** — i–♭VII–♭VI–♭VII and i–iv–v are
  classic Aeolian progressions
- **Relative minor** — A Aeolian contains exactly the same notes as C Ionian

### Practice tips

- **Hear the ♭6** — play A Aeolian over an Am chord and linger on the F
  natural; that ♭6 is what makes it sound sadder than
  [Dorian](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/dorian), which has a natural 6
- **Compare with Dorian** — this is the most important comparison; the ♭6 vs.
  natural 6 is the difference between melancholy and brightness in a minor
  context
- **Know it as relative minor** — A Aeolian = C major starting from A; be
  comfortable thinking in both directions
- **Compare with [Harmonic Minor](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/harmonic-minor)** — raising
  the ♭7 to a natural 7 creates the leading tone and the V7 chord, which is
  why harmonic minor exists
