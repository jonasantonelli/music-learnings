**Phrygian** is the 3rd mode of the [major scale](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/ionian). Its
defining feature is the **♭2** — a half step above the root — which gives it a
dark, exotic flavor associated with flamenco, metal, and Middle Eastern music.
It's the darkest of the three minor modes in the major scale family.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | ♭2 | ♭3 | 4 | 5 | ♭6 | ♭7 |
|--------|---|----|----|---|---|----|-----|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 1 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 8 | 10 |

Compared to Aeolian (natural minor), the only change is the ♭2. That half
step above the root is the entire Phrygian character.

### Chord applications

- **Sus♭9 chords** — the ♭2 over a suspended chord creates the Phrygian color
- **iii chords in a major key** — Em7 in C major naturally produces E Phrygian
- **Modal vamps** — Phrygian works beautifully over a static minor chord when
  you want a dark, Spanish sound
- **Flamenco and metal** — the ♭2 is the defining interval of both styles

### Practice tips

- **Hear the ♭2** — play E Phrygian over an Em chord and emphasize the F
  natural; that half-step pull is the whole identity of the mode
- **Compare with Aeolian** — play both back to back in the same key to isolate
  the ♭2 effect
- **Don't confuse with Phrygian Dominant** — Phrygian has a ♭3 (minor), while
  [Phrygian Dominant](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/mixolydian-b9-b13) has a natural 3 (dominant) —
  same ♭2 but very different harmonic function
- **Try over a drone** — hold a low E pedal and play E Phrygian to hear the
  mode in its purest form
