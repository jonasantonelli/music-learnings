**Lydian** is the 4th mode of the [major scale](/lessons/scales/greek-modes/ionian). It's a
major scale with a **♯4** — and that single raised note changes everything.
The ♯4 removes the half-step tension between the 3rd and 4th degrees that
exists in Ionian, creating a brighter, more open, floating quality. It's the
brightest of all seven modes.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | 3 | ♯4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
|--------|---|---|---|----|---|---|---|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 7 | 9 | 11 |

Compared to Ionian, only the 4th is raised. This eliminates the "avoid note"
problem that Ionian has over Maj7 chords — every note in Lydian sounds
consonant over a Maj7.

### Chord applications

- **Maj7♯11 chords** — Lydian is the native scale for any chord with a ♯11
- **IV chord in a major key** — FMaj7 in C major naturally produces F Lydian
- **Static Maj7 vamps** — when a tune sits on a Maj7 chord, Lydian avoids the
  natural 4 tension and sounds more open than Ionian
- **Film scoring** — the floating, dreamlike quality of Lydian is heavily used
  in cinematic music (think John Williams)

### Practice tips

- **Hear the ♯4** — play C Lydian over a Cmaj7 chord and sustain the F♯; notice
  how it floats instead of creating tension like the natural F does in Ionian
- **No avoid notes** — unlike Ionian, every note in Lydian sits comfortably over
  a Maj7 chord; this is why many jazz players default to Lydian over I chords
- **Compare with Ionian** — play both back to back; the ♯4 is the only
  difference but the mood shift is dramatic
- **Connect to [Lydian ♭7](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/lydian-b7)** — Lydian has a natural 7
  (major); Lydian ♭7 has a ♭7 (dominant). Same ♯4, different 7th
