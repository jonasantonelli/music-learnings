The **melodic minor** scale — in its jazz form — is a minor scale with a raised
6th and 7th. It sounds minor at the bottom and major at the top, which gives it
a modern, slightly ambiguous quality prized in jazz and fusion. Unlike the
classical version, jazz melodic minor keeps the same notes ascending and
descending, and its real value is as a parent scale that generates a family of
rich modal sounds.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | ♭3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
|--------|---|---|----|---|---|---|---|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 7 | 9 | 11 |

Compared to natural minor (1 2 ♭3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7), the jazz melodic minor raises
both the 6th and the 7th. Compared to the major scale, only the 3rd is
lowered — it's effectively "major with a ♭3".

### The modes of melodic minor

Melodic minor is more useful as a *parent scale* than as a standalone sound.
Each of its seven modes has its own name and application:

| Degree | Mode name | Common use |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Melodic Minor | Minor tonic, minor(maj7) chords |
| 2 | Dorian ♭2 | Sus♭9 chords |
| 3 | Lydian Augmented | Maj7♯5 chords |
| 4 | [Lydian ♭7](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/lydian-b7) | Dominant 7♯11 chords |
| 5 | Mixolydian ♭6 | Dominant 7♭13 chords |
| 6 | Locrian ♮2 | Half-diminished chords |
| 7 | Altered (Super Locrian) | Altered dominant chords |

### Chord applications

The melodic minor scale itself is most naturally played over a **minor(maj7)**
chord built on its root — this is the only common chord that contains both the
♭3 and the natural 7. It's the signature sound of James Bond themes and much of
Wayne Shorter's writing.

In practice, jazz players rarely *improvise* on plain melodic minor for long
stretches; they reach for it to outline a minor(maj7), or more often to pull a
mode from it (most commonly the altered scale over a V7, or Lydian ♭7 over a
non-functional dominant).

### Practice tips

- **Learn it against its parent feel** — play C melodic minor over a Cm(maj7)
  chord and really hear the natural 7 resolving
- **Know which mode comes from which degree** — the table above is worth
  memorizing; it's the key to unlocking the whole family
- **Compare with natural minor** — play both scales back to back in the same
  key to internalize the sound of the raised 6th and 7th
- **Don't treat it as a finger pattern** — it only becomes useful when you
  hear which mode of it you're applying to the current chord
