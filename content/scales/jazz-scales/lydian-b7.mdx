**Lydian ♭7** — also called **Lydian Dominant** — is the 4th mode of
[melodic minor](/lessons/scales/jazz-scales/melodic-minor). It's a major scale with a
♯4 and a ♭7, which makes it the natural scale choice over dominant 7 chords
that *don't want to resolve* — non-functional dominants, tritone substitutions,
and modal vamps. It sounds bright and floating, never tense.

### Interval formula

| Degree | 1 | 2 | 3 | ♯4 | 5 | 6 | ♭7 |
|--------|---|---|---|----|---|---|----|
| Semitones from root | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 7 | 9 | 10 |

Relative to Mixolydian (the usual dominant scale, 1 2 3 4 5 6 ♭7), the only
change is raising the 4th to ♯4. That single note is the whole flavor — it
introduces the ♯11 color and removes the avoid-note friction Mixolydian has
over a dominant chord.

### Relation to melodic minor

Lydian ♭7 is the **4th mode of melodic minor**. C Lydian ♭7 contains the same
notes as **G melodic minor** — just starting from C instead of G:

> G melodic minor: G A B♭ C D E F♯
> C Lydian ♭7: C D E F♯ G A B♭

This is the most practical relationship to memorize: **to play Lydian ♭7 on
any dominant chord, start from the melodic minor scale a perfect 5th below the
root** (or, equivalently, a perfect 4th above).

### Chord applications

Lydian ♭7 is the go-to scale over any dominant chord where the ♯11 is
welcome. The main cases:

- **Non-functional dominant 7 chords** — a V7 chord that doesn't resolve to I,
  like the ♭VII7 in modal tunes (F7 in C Dorian), or the bII7 bluesy substitute
- **Tritone substitutions** — when a V7 is subbed for the tritone-sub ♭II7
  (e.g. D♭7 replacing G7 going to C), the ♭II7 is almost always voiced as
  a 7♯11, and Lydian ♭7 is its native scale
- **Dominant 7♯11 chords** — any time you see the ♯11 written in the chord
  symbol, this is your scale
- **Static dominant vamps** — funk and fusion tunes that sit on a single
  dominant chord for bars at a time; Lydian ♭7 sounds far less tense than
  Mixolydian here

A classic exercise: play a C7♯11 vamp and improvise with the notes of G
melodic minor. Every note you land on will sound "right" without needing
resolution.

### Practice tips

- **Hear the ♯4 against the ♭7** — that tritone between the 3rd and the ♯4
  (and again between the ♯4 and ♭7) is what gives the scale its
  floating character
- **Practice against a single dominant chord** — hold a C7 drone and play
  C Lydian ♭7 over it; notice how the ♯11 doesn't beg to resolve
- **Connect it to its parent** — be able to instantly switch between
  "C Lydian ♭7" and "G melodic minor" in your mind; they're the same notes
- **Try tritone-sub ii-V-I** — over a Dm7 – D♭7 – Cmaj7 progression, play
  D♭ Lydian ♭7 (= A♭ melodic minor) on the D♭7 bar
